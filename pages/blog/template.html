Uncovering Crediton’s Past: A New Discovery

March 15, 2025 | By Jane Doe

  1. Home
  2. Blog
  3. Article Title Here
← ← Previous Article → Next Article →

A recently uncovered photograph of a pioneer family, circa 1850s.

Last month, while sifting through a donated collection of family papers, our team stumbled upon a remarkable find: a photograph and journal from one of Crediton’s earliest settlers. This discovery sheds new light on the community’s founding in the 1850s and reaffirms the importance of preserving our shared history.

The Find

The photograph, faded but intact, depicts a family standing in front of a log cabin—likely one of the Stahl or Fahrner families, given their prominence among Crediton’s German settlers. Accompanying it was a journal page dated 1852, detailing the challenges of clearing land in Stephen Township. The entry reads: “The forest is thick, but each tree felled brings us closer to home.”

"The forest is thick, but each tree felled brings us closer to home." – Journal Entry, 1852

This artifact not only personalizes the pioneer experience but also connects directly to Crediton Cemetery, where many of these early settlers, like Barbara Shulz (d. 1835), are buried.

Why It Matters

Findings like these remind us that history is not static—it evolves with each new story we uncover. They enrich our virtual exhibits, such as “Pioneers of Crediton,” and deepen our understanding of the resilience that built this community. We’re now working to digitize and preserve these items for public access.

Do you have old photos, letters, or stories from Crediton’s past? Your contributions could be the next key to unlocking our history. Get in touch with us to share!
